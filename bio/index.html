Books of 2022

I am sad to admit I have not been reading as much as I used to, but I am hoping to pick it up more this year! My reading list is growing too long and I do miss it. These are all either books I am currently reading or finished this year.

  • Blood River Rising: The Thompson-Crismon feud of the 1920s

    Victoria Pope Hubbell

    A book recommended to me by my mother- it tells the story of a family feud escalating to the point of murder brought about, largely, by the arrival of the KKK in a tiny, effectively all white farming town. It was of special interest to us because I grew up within a few miles of the events of the book.

  • The Science of Accelerated Learning

    Peter Hollins

    A quick read, and one with simple grounded rules for learning. I appreciate that it disproves the notion of 'types' of learners and focuses on what I would call (oversimplified) 'interaction' in learning. Whether by repeating out loud, or writing down/sorting/reviewing you must be actively engaged in order to retain information efficiently.

  • The Gulag Archipelago Volume 1

    Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn

    If you are unfamiliar with this work, you should rectify that immediately.

  • Blackberries in the Dream House

    Diane Frank

    "What would happen to us if we were to undertake the discipline of turning our life entirely and self-consciously into a poem? Through Yukiko, who becomes both a contemplative Buddhist and a geisha skilled in the refinements of sensuous pleasure, Diane Frank allows us to live within the soul of a young woman who has undertaken to create a life imagined and expressed as a poem, in every moment, waking and sleeping, making love or meditating." - Pierre DeLattre

  • The Warmth of Other Suns: The Epic Story of America's Great Migration

    Isabel Wilkerson

    A narrative exploration of the migration of ~6 million african-americans to the north from 1915 to 1970. The fictional characters are spread in location and time, and are strongly influenced by the actual histories of over 1000 people interviewed for this book.

  • Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind

    Yuval Noah Harari

    My only audio book, and one I have heard a lot of criticism on regarding authorial bias and broad, shallow strokes. I understand, and probably even agree with most of that criticism, but I still find the work very engaging. To expound: Even if you don't agree that wheat tricked man into indenturing itself to long hours in the field- the notion itself is nonetheless fascinating.
    (The example is admittedly perhaps too benign- the author continues through slavery, capitalism, and colonialism and on ground that is already shaking.)

  • Iliad

    Homer

    I have read very few 'classic novels' or 'great works'. I never had a literature class which seems quite odd in retrospect (given my adoration of reading). Post-college I still avoided them because many, I felt, were quite impenetrable without guided study and discourse. I still feel that way to an extent but my Homeric translation is easy to read and my girlfriend's mother is a classics professor so the many questions evoked by the text regarding greek culture and mindset are quickly answered. Thank you professor!

  • The Broken Earth Trilogy

    N. K. Jemisin

    I cannot seem to describe this book without an egregious quantity of spoilers so instead I'll say this- I started the first book and promptly dropped everything else I was reading until I had finished the series.

  • The Beast That Shouted Love at the Heart of the World

    Harlan Ellison

    Stories of the future (and the now, and when, and everywhere) where people are too much the same. Plus the true story of Santa Claus.

  • The Lord Of The Rings Trilogy

    J.R.R. Tolkien

    Its been over 20 years since I've read these books. Its astonishing to me that they are as enjoyable now, if for completely different reasons, as they were back then.

  • Amusing Ourselves to Death

    Neil Postman

    How many episodes of Last Week Tonight have I watched without taking any real action after? This work left me feeling very troubled after reading, and that is definitely to be interpreted as praise.

  • Caves of Steel

    Isaac Asimov

    I could have sworn I wasn't interested in murder mystery novels...

  • The Letters of Anne Gilchrist and Walt Whitman

    Anne Burrows; Walt Whitman

  • The Song Of The Cell

    Siddhartha Mukherjee

    Emergency purchased to get through a 3 hour flight delay and I'm so glad I found it. A narrative exploration of the discovery of and treatments designed for cells.

Interests
